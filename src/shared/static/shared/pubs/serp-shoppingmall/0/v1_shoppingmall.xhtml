Should Shopping Malls Be Allowed To Institute Teen Curfews?

Gina and her friends just lost their social life. Their local mall instituted a 6:00 p.m. curfew for anyone under 16 years old. Before the new curfew, their parents used to drop Gina and her friends off at the mall every Friday night. They met up with classmates and hung out. Now, the teens have to bring an adult. Forty-six of 1,200 U.S. malls have instituted parental-escort policies. More malls are likely to do the same. They want to invoke parental responsibility to watch teen behavior in malls.

Curfew Laws

State and local governments usually decide curfew law in the United States. Those laws risk being overruled if they go against Constitutional rights. Governments design these laws to keep order and prevent inappropriate behavior. Sometimes the military uses curfews to encourage young soldiers to follow good behaviors.

Malls are a common place for teens to hang out after school. Teens like being in the malls. Malls provide a fun place to get together with friends from school. They are a good place to meet students from neighboring schools. Many believe the mall is a safe and well-monitored environment for young people. Most malls value teens as consumers. Store owners know teens have buying power. One recent study found that on a typical visit, 68% of teens spent two or more hours in the mall. More than half of teens surveyed spent $50 or more during their last visit. Also, mall managers realize that teens are their future adult customers. They do not want to discourage teens from coming to the mall.

However, malls were not designed to be babysitters for teenagers. Unfortunately, many malls have been forced to take on this role. One mall reported that misbehaving teens would hang over the railings. Sometimes teens would drop food or spit on customers below. At the same mall, two fifteen-year-olds fought over a pair of shoes. One of them was almost pushed over the railing. On another night, two gangs had a scuffle. One gang member had a gun and aimed it at an innocent bystander. After starting the parental-escort policy, the Mall of America, the largest mall in the country, reported a decrease from 300 to only 2 incidents involving bad behavior by teens.

Challenges to the Laws

In recent years there have been many challenges to curfew laws. The American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) has participated in these challenges. ACLU spokespersons always want to protect the rights of people according to the Constitution. Their main complaints regard the hard to understand, or unclear, wording of the laws and the manner in which they are enforced. They feel that officers often target young people based on race.

Big groups of teens who yell and fight create discomfort in older customers. Therefore, many older customers began to avoid the mall on Friday and Saturday nights. Since older customers have more money than most teens, mall managers want to encourage them to come back to the mall. One mall reported a 29% increase in revenue on Friday and Saturday nights after the curfew was started.
