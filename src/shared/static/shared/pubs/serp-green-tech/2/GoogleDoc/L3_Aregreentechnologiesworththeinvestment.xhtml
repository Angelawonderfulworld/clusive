Are green technologies worth the investment?

As Amanda walked through the park, she noticed a big new trash can called a BigBelly. When she researched this new technology on the web, it turned out that this trash can costs $6,000! The BigBelly contains a solar-powered compactor inside, so it maximizes the quantity of trash contained in the can while diminishing the number of times it needs to be emptied. The BigBelly is one of many “green technologies ” now available. However, does it make sense to invest so much money in them? City managers and mayors say that the BigBelly is worth the investment, as each BigBelly will pay for itself in three years because the cans only need to be emptied once a week. Traditional trash cans get emptied once or twice a day at a cost of about $2,000 a year each, and the trucks that travel around to empty them use gasoline and contaminate the atmosphere. Nevertheless, buying a lot of BigBelly cans could be a costly expenditure for in any city’s budget! Wind turbines and solar energy are types of green technologies. These wind turbines capture the energy of the wind and convert it into electricity.

The BigBelly uses a renewable energy source, solar energy from the sun. Currently, there are many other renewable technologies emerging  as well. Consumers are installing solar panels on their roofs and converting to solar-powered electricity from electricity produced with coal or gas.  Solar-powered cars and planes are under development. Solar-powered light strips have been situated on some rural highways; once in place, the light strips run for free and rarely require supervision . Investors are putting funds into developing solar panels that may someday replace the asphalt on highways; these panels use solar power to generate heat, so that ice and snow melt immediately, light, so that streetlamps are unnecessary, and additional electricity that nearby towns and cities will be able to use.

Investment in these solar technologies is extremely costly. The Department of Transportation gave $100,000 to the solar pavement project, but several million more will be needed just to finish the development. Are such expensive technologies worth this costly investment? Should we proceed to invest in technologies that may never be operational on a large scale? Shouldn’t we conserve public funds for more urgent and immediate needs, like improving schools and fixing potholes? What if we develop green technologies but are deficient in the funds to proceed with using them? How high a price should we pay for green technology?
