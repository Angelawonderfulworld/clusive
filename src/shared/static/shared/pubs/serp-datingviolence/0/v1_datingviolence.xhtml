Dating Violence: Who is Responsible?

In the hallway of Stevens High School, Franny and Jada, two seventh-grade girls, lean against their lockers. They are reading a magazine. They read a horrible story about their favorite pop singing pair: Shelley and James. Jada reads aloud: “James became angry at Shelley. He hit her in the face. Then he punched and bit her repeatedly. He threatened to kill her. He even choked her. He did this for over 20 minutes.” At first, neither girl can believe that James physically assaulted Shelley. “Maybe Shelley is lying,” Jada says. “Maybe Shelley wasn’t hurt that badly. Or maybe she did something that triggered the assault.” When they see the photo of Shelley, they change their minds. Shelly’s face is bruised and bloody.

According to the Family Violence Prevention Fund, dating violence is a significant problem. One in three teenage girls is physically, emotionally, or verbally abused by a dating partner.

Franny and Jada read on. The article says that when James was young, he saw his father beat his mother. His opinion toward women and violence may have been distorted by these experiences. But is this a good explanation? Does it excuse his behavior?

Finally, the article says that Shelley someday forgave James. She dropped the charges. Franny and Jada are outraged. “Even if Shelley drops the charges, the police should make James pay,” says Franny. “He broke the law!” Franny’s and Jada’s friend, Kim, overhears their conversation. “No one really knows what went on between Shelley and James,” she says. “If Shelley wants him back, that’s her business.”
