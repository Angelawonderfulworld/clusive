Dating Violence: Who is Responsible?

Franny and Jada, two seventh-grade girls, stand in the hallway of Stevens High School, leaning against their lockers perusing a magazine. They read a shocking story about their favorite pop singing twosome: Shelley and James. Jada reads the story aloud: “James became enraged at Shelley. He struck her in the face, then punched and bit her repeatedly, while he threatened to kill her. James even attempted to throttle Jada, and the terrible abuse lasted for over 20 minutes.” At first, the girls were astounded; neither girl can believe that James, the beloved star, could have physically assaulted Shelley. “Maybe Shelley is lying,” Jada says. “Maybe Shelley wasn’t injured that severely. Or maybe she precipitated the event; maybe she did something that triggered the assault.” However, when Franny and Jada see the photo of Shelley’s bruised and bloodied face, they changed their minds.

According to the Family Violence Prevention Fund, dating violence is a significant problem. One in three teenage girls is physically, emotionally, or verbally abused by a dating partner.

Franny and Jada continue to read further on in the article. The article says that when James was a youngster, he saw his father beat his mother. His perspective toward women and violence may have become distorted by these experiences. But is this an adequate explanation? Does it excuse his behavior?

Finally, the article says that Shelley ultimately forgave James and dropped the charges she had filed against him. Franny and Jada are outraged. “Even if Shelley drops the charges, the police should make James pay,” says Franny. “He broke the law!” Franny’s and Jada’s friend, Kim, overhears their conversation. “No one really knows what went on between Shelley and James,” she says. “If Shelley wants him back, that’s her business.”

What do you think? Should Shelley press charges, or is it her right to decide that James should be forgiven? Is dating violence a confidential matter, or a public one? How should we handle dating violence?
