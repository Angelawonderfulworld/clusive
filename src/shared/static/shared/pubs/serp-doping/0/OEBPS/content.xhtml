Who is responsible for doping in professional team sports?

Why do so many well-known athletes use drugs to help them play better? People accuse baseball players like Manuel “Manny” Ramírez, Roger Clemens, and Alex Rodriguez of using steroids. People also accuse players of using outlawed drugs (“doping”). They use these to help their athletic performance. Ramírez retired in 2011. He left baseball to avoid a 100-game suspension after he tested positive for performance-enhancing drugs. It was the third time testing positive for drugs in his career.

Alex Rodriguez, a slugger for the New York Yankees, was suspended for the 2014 baseball season. He had used prohibited performance-enhancing drugs. Rodriguez denied the use of chemical compounds. Even though he denies using drugs, he has been disgraced and people still doubt him. 

Steroids are illegal for athletes to use. They are easily found through blood tests. Now athletes are substituting new ways of cheating. These new ways are harder to find.  For example, cyclists and other endurance athletes give themselves blood transfusions. This changes the amount of oxygen in their blood.

In 2013, the cyclist Lance Armstrong admitted to doping. Doping helped Armstrong win many titles and trophies. It helped him get a lot of money and endorsement deals. Armstrong won the Tour de France seven times. After evidence surfaced of his use of illegal compounds, the United States Anti-Doping Agency took his titles away. The U.S. Postal Service is even suing Armstrong for fraud. They gave millions of dollars to help him and his team. They felt cheated, too.

Some people say that athletes should not be blamed for doing doping. Sports writers point out that athletes are pressured to perform and do better than competitors. That makes steroid use tempting. Fans and sponsors push for more power hitting, faster times, and bigger championships. If they did not, maybe athletes wouldn't be so likely to alter their bodies with dangerous drugs.  

 Many athletes are conflicted about using these drugs. Drugs hurt athletes’ health. Athletes using steroids know the negative costs. Men's breasts grow and their testicles shrink. They may get acne. Many users experience confusion or feelings of anger, known as “roid rage.”

Some fundamental questions that should be asked are: Who is responsible for making illegal substances part of team sports? Would athletes alter their blood and body chemistry if they didn't feel so much pressure to outperform their peers? Should fans and sponsors bear some of the responsibility?
