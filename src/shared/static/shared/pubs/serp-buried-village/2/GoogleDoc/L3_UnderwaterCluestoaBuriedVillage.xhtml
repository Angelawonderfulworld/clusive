Underwater Clues to a Buried Village

Crawford Lake is a small, deep lake west of Toronto. Lakes of this kind, called “meromictic lakes,” are found all over the world, but they are uncommon and of special interest to many scientists. Why?

​Investigating below the surface can reveal clues to the past.

In most lakes, all the water mixes together and churns up the mud on the bottom. In meromictic lakes, the water is so vastly deep that the mud on the bottom doesn’t get disarranged. Things that fall into the lake sink to the bottom, pile up and stay there for thousands of years, forming layers that can be seen. Because of this layering, palaeobotanists (scientists who study fossilized plants) can take samples of bottom mud and make discoveries about the plants that lived on the shores of the lake long ago.

That’s just what was happening at Crawford Lake in 1971. A palaeobotanist decided to collect a representative section of bottom mud in order to study the pollen grains  it possessed. By looking at the fossilized pollen in the lake mud, he hoped to learn what plants had grown near the lake for hundreds of years and discover how those plants had changed over time.

To collect bottom mud, the botanist filled a heavy hollow tube with dry ice to make it freezing cold. He lowered this “frigid finger” into the lake and into the mud, and when he pulled it up ten minutes later, it was coated with a thin crust of frozen mud.

Back at the lab, the botanist and his assistant studied the frozen crust. They could see layers, two for each year. The top two layers were from 1971. The further down they went, the older the layers and the pollen were.

Then they found something that they did not expect. The mud layers that were about 530 years old contained a great deal of corn pollen. Initially, the botanist didn’t believe the findings. Why would corn pollen suddenly show up in the middle of a forest? Was it possible that native people had once lived on the shores of the lake, cut down the trees and planted corn fields? The botanist went to an archaeologist for help.

How do you find the remnants of a community that vanished centuries ago? If you’re an archaeologist, you look for signs that people have somehow changed the land, changes that humans make, such as telltale ridges or bumps or hollows from where they have built or moved the dirt, rocks, or land. You watch for artifacts dug up by groundhogs or by a farmer’s plough . And you go to people who know the land better than you do, people such as farmers.

It turned out that the farmer who had lived on the shores of Crawford Lake had collected a basketful of stone axes and arrowheads on his land, most of which he had found in his barnyard. Sure enough, when archaeologists dug up the barnyard, they found pottery, food storage pits, stains from old firepits and marks in the earth from long house posts . And in the garbage pits, they found charred kernels of corn! The botanist’s guess was right. Native people had once lived on the shores of the lake and had grown corn.
